## Early Architectures

### LeNet-5 (1998) [LeCun et al. (1998) [@LeCun98]]

LeNet‑5 was one of the first convolutional neural networks (CNNs) designed for handwritten digit recognition on the MNIST dataset.  
The architecture processes a $32\times32$ grayscale input image through a regular alternation of convolutional layers, subsampling (pooling) layers, and finally a multilayer perceptron (MLP) that produces a 10‑way classification output.

* **Convolutional layers C1, C3, C5** – These are displayed as rectangular blocks whose dimensions encode the number of feature maps and their spatial resolution. For example, the first convolutional layer C1 is denoted as $6@28\times28$, meaning that six feature maps of size $28\times28$ are produced. Convolution extracts local spatial patterns with learned kernels.  

* **Subsampling layers S2, S4, S6** – Subsampling (average pooling in the original design) reduces the spatial dimensions of the feature maps, e.g. from $28\times28$ (after C1) to $14\times14$ (after S2). This operation provides translational invariance and decreases the number of parameters for the subsequent layers.  

* **Sparse connectivity between S2 and C3** – Unlike a fully connected feed‑forward scheme, the original LeNet‑5 uses a hand‑crafted sparse connection pattern in which each map of C3 receives input from only a subset of the maps of S2. This reduces computation, improves memory efficiency, and contributes to robustness.  

* **Non‑linearity** – After each convolution and subsampling operation the network applies the hyperbolic tangent activation $\tanh(\cdot)$. At the time, $\tanh$ was the standard smooth, bounded non‑linearity.  

* **Final MLP (layers F6 and Gaussian connections)** – The last two layers are fully connected. Layer F6 contains 84 neurons and is followed by a Gaussian‑connected output layer that implements a softmax‑like classifier for the ten digit classes.

The overall processing pipeline can be described as the sequence *convolution → average pooling → non‑linearity* repeated three times, followed by a fully connected classifier. This simple yet effective template became the foundation for many later CNN architectures, and the same basic building blocks (convolution, pooling, non‑linearity, and a final classifier) are still present in modern designs.

### AlexNet (2012) [Krizhevsky et al. (2012) [@Krizhevsky12]]

AlexNet dramatically revived interest in deep convolutional networks by winning the ImageNet Large‑Scale Visual Recognition Challenge 2012, roughly halving the previous state-of-the-art error rate. The network consists of eight layers in total: five convolutional layers followed by three dense (fully connected) layers. The input is a color image of size $3\times224\times224$. Historically, the architecture was split into two parallel branches because the GPUs available at the time were too small to hold the entire network, with each GPU handling roughly half of the computations.

#### Architectural overview

1. **First convolutional layer** – The input passes through a convolution with 48 filters of size $11\times11$ and stride 4, yielding an output of size $48\times55\times55$. This aggressive stride reduces the spatial resolution early, limiting computational cost.  

2. **Subsequent convolutional layers** – Layers 2 through 5 continue to apply convolutions with smaller receptive fields ($5\times5$ or $3\times3$). After selected convolutions the network performs *max pooling* with a $3\times3$ window and stride 2 (overlapping pooling), which further halves the spatial dimensions while retaining the strongest activations.  

3. **Fully connected classifier** – The flattened feature map is fed into three dense layers with sizes $2048$, $2048$, and $1000$, respectively. The final 1000‑unit layer corresponds to the number of ImageNet categories.  

4. **Skip connections** – Dotted arrows in the diagram indicate that some layers receive inputs from non‑adjacent earlier layers, a precursor to modern residual connections.

#### Techniques for combating overfitting

* **Dropout** – During training, each of the first two fully connected layers randomly drops units with probability $p=0.5$. This stochastic regularization prevents co‑adaptation of neurons and reduces overfitting.  

* **Data augmentation** – The training images are randomly transformed (e.g., random crops, horizontal flips, color jitter) and subjected to random intensity variations. This artificially enlarges the dataset and encourages the network to learn invariances to such perturbations.

#### Optimization and regularization details

* **Mini‑batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with momentum** – AlexNet uses a batch size of 128, momentum coefficient $0.9$, and an $L_2$ weight decay of $5\times10^{-5}$. Momentum accelerates convergence by smoothing the gradient updates.  

* **Weight initialization** – All weights are drawn from a zero‑mean Gaussian distribution with standard deviation $0.01$, i.e. $\mathcal{N}(0,0.01)$. This modest variance prevents early saturation of $\tanh$ or ReLU activations.  

* **ReLU non‑linearity** – After each convolution and fully connected layer a rectified linear unit $\operatorname{ReLU}(x)=\max(0,x)$ is applied. ReLU accelerates training because its gradient does not vanish for positive inputs.  

* **GPU acceleration** – The model was trained on two NVIDIA GPUs, each handling roughly half of the network’s parameters. This parallelism reduced the training time from weeks (on CPUs) to days, demonstrating the importance of hardware for deep learning breakthroughs.

AlexNet’s combination of deeper architecture, ReLU activations, dropout, data augmentation, and massive GPU computation set the standard for subsequent CNN designs.

### Network In Network [Lin et al. (2014) [@Lin14]]

Network‑in‑Network (NiN) replaces the linear convolutional filters used in traditional CNNs with micro multilayer perceptrons implemented as $1\times1$ convolutions. Each block of the architecture contains:

* **Convolutional layers with $1\times1$ kernels** – A $1\times1$ convolution can be interpreted as a fully connected layer applied independently at every spatial location. Because the same weights are shared across all locations, the operation introduces non‑linear mixing of the channel dimension while adding only a negligible number of parameters.  

* **Non‑linear activation after each $1\times1$ convolution** – Stacking multiple $1\times1$ convolutions interleaved with non‑linearities enables the network to learn complex, non‑linear functions of the local receptive field, overcoming the linearity limitation of ordinary convolutions.  

* **Max‑pooling layers** – After a stack of $1\times1$ convolutions, a max‑pooling layer reduces spatial resolution, providing translation invariance.  

* **Global average pooling** – Instead of a final fully connected classifier, NiN applies a spatial average over each feature map, producing a single scalar per channel. This “global average pooling” dramatically reduces the number of parameters in the classifier and acts as a strong regularizer, making the network less prone to overfitting.

The NiN design therefore achieves high expressive power with far fewer parameters than a conventional conv‑plus‑FC architecture, and it introduces the idea of learning channel‑wise interactions via $1\times1$ filters—an idea that later reappears in Inception modules and residual bottlenecks.

### VGG Network (Visual Geometry Group – University of Oxford) [Simonyan & Zisserman (2015) [@Simonyan14]]

The VGG family, particularly VGG‑16, demonstrated that depth alone (with simple, homogeneous building blocks) can substantially improve performance on large‑scale image classification.

#### Structural characteristics

* **Uniform $3\times3$ convolution kernels** – Every convolutional layer uses a $3\times3$ filter with stride 1 and padding 1, preserving spatial dimensions. Stacking multiple $3\times3$ convolutions yields an effective receptive field equivalent to larger kernels (e.g., two stacked $3\times3$ convolutions approximate a $5\times5$ receptive field) while using fewer parameters.  

* **Depth** – VGG‑16 comprises 13 convolutional layers followed by three fully connected layers (total 16 weight layers). VGG‑19 adds two more convolutions, reaching 19 weight layers.  

* **Interleaved max‑pooling** – After each block of convolutions, a $2\times2$ max‑pooling operation with stride 2 halves the spatial resolution (e.g., $224\times224 \rightarrow 112\times112 \rightarrow 56\times56$, etc.).  

* **Fully connected classifier** – The final three layers are dense: two layers of 4096 units each, followed by a softmax layer with 1000 outputs for ImageNet classification.  

* **Training protocol** – The learning procedure mirrors that of AlexNet (SGD with momentum, ReLU activations, weight decay). However, training VGG networks from scratch was found to be highly unstable due to their depth. Consequently, practitioners typically pre‑train shallower variants (e.g., VGG‑11)

This is very helpful, in particular, in things like ImageNet where you have rather noisy labels. So, you remember the cases that were not entirely clear. In these noisy label cases, you can see that this label smoothing can really help. The idea is that you multiply your Dirac distribution with one minus some small number ϵ. You then distribute the ϵ that you deducted from the correct class to all the other classes in an equal distribution. So you can see here that is simply 1/K where K is the number of classes. The nice thing about this label smoothing is that you essentially discourage very hard decisions. This really helps in the case of noisy labels. So this is a very nice trick that can help you with building deeper models.

If you want to train deeper models with all the things that we’ve seen so far, you see that we go into a certain kind of saturation. We see that if you try to build those very deep models, we get problems with vanishing gradients and we can’t train early layers which even results in a worse loss on the training set.

So, I have one solution for you and these are residual units. They are a very cool idea. So, what they propose to do is not learn the direct mapping F(x), but instead, we learn the residual mapping. So, we want to learn H(x) and H(x) is the difference between F(x) and x. So, we can also express it in a different way and this is actually how it’s implemented. You compute your network F(x) as some layer H(x) + x. So, the trainable part is now essentially in a side branch and the side branch is H(x) that’s the trainable one. In the original implementation of residual blocks, there was still a difference. It was not exactly like we had it on the previous slide. So we had the side branch where we have a weighting layer, batch normalization, ReLU, weighting, another batch normalization, followed by the addition and another non-linearity, the ReLU for one residual block. This was later then changed into using batch norm, ReLU, weight, batch norm, ReLU, and weight for the residual block. It turned out that this kind of configuration was more stable. We essentially have the identity that is backpropagated on the plus branch. So, this is very nice because we can then propagate the gradient back into the early layers just because of this addition and we get a much more stable backpropagation this way.

This then brings us to a complete residual network. So, we cut it at the bottom and show the bottom part on the right-hand side. So, this is why we have dotted connections at these positions. We can see that VGG has some 19.6 billion FLOPs while our plain and residual networks have only 3.6 billion FLOPs. There are different variants of the residual block networks. People have been asking “Why do these residual networks perform so well?”. A very interesting interpretation is this so-called ensemble view of ResNets. # Lecture Notes in Deep Learning: Architectures – Part 5

## Learning Architectures

These are the lecture notes for FAU’s YouTube Lecture “ Deep Learning “. This is a full transcript of the lecture video & matching slides. We hope, you enjoy this as much as the videos. Of course, this transcript was created with deep learning techniques largely automatically and only minor manual modifications were performed. If you spot mistakes, please let us know!

Welcome back to deep learning and today we want to talk about the final part of the architectures. In particular, we want to look into learning architectures. Part 5: Learning Architectures. Well, the idea here is that we want to have self-developing network structures and they can be optimized with respect to accuracy or floating-point operations. Of course, you could simply do that with a grid search. But typically that’s too time-consuming.

So, there have been a couple of approaches to do that. One of the ideas here in [22] is using reinforcement learning. So, you train a recurrent neural network (RNN) to generate model descriptions of networks and you train this RNN with reinforcement learning to maximize the expected accuracy. Of course, there are also many other options. You can do reinforcement learning for small building blocks transfer to large CNNs and genetic algorithms, energy-based ones, and there’s actually plenty of ideas that you could follow. They are all very expensive in terms of training time and if you want to look into those approaches, you really have to have a large cluster. Otherwise, you aren’t able to actually perform the experiments. So, there are actually not too many groups in the world that are able to do such kind of research right now.

So you can see that also here many elements that we’ve seen earlier pop up again. There are separable convolutions and many other blocks here on the left-hand side. You see this normal cell kind of looks like an inception block. If you look at the right-hand side it kind of looks like later versions of the inception modules where you have these separations. They are somehow concatenated and also use residual connections. This has been determined only by architecture search. Performance for ImageNet is on par with the squeeze and excitation networks with lower computational costs. There are also, of course, optimizations possible for different sizes for example for mobile platforms.

ImageNet – Where are we? Well, we see that the ImageNet classification has dropped now below 5% in most of the submissions. Substantial and significant improvements are more and more difficult to show on this data set. The last official challenge was on CVPR in 2017. It’s now continued on Kaggle. There are new data sets that are being generated and are needed. For example, 3-D scenes, human-level understanding, and those data sets are currently being generated. there are for example things like the MS COCO dataset or the visual genome data set which have replaced ImageNet as state-of-the-art data set. Of course, there are also different research directions like speed and size of networks for mobile applications. In these situations, ImageNet may still be a suitable challenge.

So, let’s come to some conclusions. We see that the 1×1 filters reduce the parameters and add regularization. It is a very common technique. Inception modules are really nice because they allow you to find the right balance between convolution and pooling. The residual connections are a recipe that has been used over and over again. We’ve also seen that some of the new architectures can actually be learned. So, we see that there is a rise of deeper models from five layers to more than a thousand. However, often a smaller net is sufficient. Of course, this depends on the amount of training data. You can only train those really big networks if you have sufficient data. We’ve seen that sometimes it also makes sense to build wider layers instead of deep layers. You remember, we’ve already seen that in the universal approximation theorem. If we had infinitely wide layers, then maybe we could fit everything into a single layer.

Okay, that brings us already to the outlook on the next couple of videos. What we want to talk about are recurrent neural networks. We will look into long short-term memory cells, into truncated backpropagation through time which is a key element in order to be able to train those recurrent networks, and we finally have a look at the long short-term memory cell. One of the key ideas that have been driven by Schmidthuber and Hochreiter. Another idea that came up by Cho is the gated recurrent unit which can somehow be a bridge between the traditional recurrent cells and the long short-term memory cells.

Well, let’s look at some comprehensive questions: So what are the advantages of deeper models in comparison to shallow networks? Why can we say that residual networks learn an ensemble of shallow networks? You remember, I hinted on that slide this is a very important concept if you want to prepare for the exam. Of course, you should be able to describe bottleneck layers, or what is the standard inception module and how can it be improved? I have a lot of further reading dual-path networks . So you can also have a look at the squeeze and excitation networks paper . There are more interesting works that can be found here on medium and of course mobile nets and other deep networks without residual connections . This already now brings us to the end of this lecture and I hope you had fun. Looking forward to seeing you in the next video where we talk about recurrent neural networks. I heard they can be written down in five lines of code. So see you next time!
