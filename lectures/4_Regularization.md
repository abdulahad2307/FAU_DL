# Regularization

## Introduction to Regularization

### Fitting appropriately

In a binary classification setting we are given a set of input vectors $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ together with class labels. Figure 1 shows a two‑dimensional scatter plot where each point belongs to one of two classes, illustrated by blue circles (class 0) and red circles (class 1). The points are not placed on a regular grid, and no axis labels are shown; the purpose of the figure is to convey the spatial distribution of the two classes. Visually there is a tendency for the blue points to occupy the upper‑left region while the red points concentrate toward the lower‑right region, but there is noticeable overlap. A learning algorithm seeks a decision function $f(\mathbf{x})$ whose zero level set $f(\mathbf{x})=0$ separates the two classes as cleanly as possible.

#### Underfitting

Figure 2 depicts the same data together with a **horizontal** black line that is used as a decision boundary. Most red points lie below the line and most blue points lie above it, yet a substantial number of points are mis‑classified because the line is too simple to capture the structure of the data. This situation illustrates **underfitting**: the hypothesis class (here a linear model) has insufficient capacity to represent the true relationship between $\mathbf{x}$ and the class label, leading to high bias and poor predictive performance, especially near the decision surface.

#### Overfitting

Figure 3 presents the data with a highly irregular black curve that snakes around individual data points. The curve follows the training data very closely, weaving between red and blue points, and even adapts to apparent noise. While the training error may be close to zero, such a model is unlikely to generalize to new examples because it has captured idiosyncratic fluctuations rather than the underlying pattern. This phenomenon is known as **overfitting**, i.e., a model with excessive capacity that exhibits low bias but high variance.

#### Sensible boundary

Figure 4 shows the same point cloud together with a **smooth, curved** black line that roughly separates the two clusters while tolerating a modest amount of overlap. The boundary is complex enough to capture the dominant structure of the data but not so flexible that it tracks every individual point. This represents a **sensible** decision surface: it balances the need to reduce bias (by being non‑linear) against the desire to keep variance low (by remaining smooth). In supervised learning we aim to learn a function $f(\mathbf{x})$ whose zero‑level set approximates this sensible boundary.

### Bias Variance Decomposition

Consider a regression problem as described in Bishop [Bishop (2006) [@Bishop]]. Let the true target value be generated by

\[
y = h(\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon , \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\epsilon}) ,
\]

When we train a model on a dataset $D$, we obtain a predictor $\hat{\mathbf{y}}_D = \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}\mid D)$. The expected squared loss for a single input $\mathbf{x}$, taken over all possible training sets $D$, is

\[
\mathbb{E}_D\!\bigl(\ell(\mathbf{x})\bigr) = \mathbb{E}_D\!\bigl[\,(h(\mathbf{x}) - \hat{\mathbf{y}}_D)^2\,\bigr] .
\]

This expected loss can be decomposed into three additive terms:

\[
\mathbb{E}_D\!\bigl(\ell(\mathbf{x})\bigr) =
\underbrace{\bigl(\,\mathbb{E}_D[\hat{\mathbf{y}}_D] - h(\mathbf{x})\,\bigr)^{2}}_{\text{Bias}^{2}} \;
+\;
\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_D\!\bigl[(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_D - \mathbb{E}_D[\hat{\mathbf{y}}_D])^{2}\bigr]}_{\text{Variance}} \;
+\;
\underbrace{\sigma_{\epsilon}^{2}}_{\text{Irreducible error}} .
\]

The **bias** term measures the deviation of the expected model prediction from the true underlying function; it quantifies how far the model is from the ground truth on average. The **variance** term captures the sensitivity of the model to fluctuations in the training data, reflecting the limited size of the dataset and the model's flexibility. The **irreducible error** arises from inherent noise in the data that no model can eliminate. 

Integrating this expression over the entire input distribution yields the overall expected loss $L_D(\mathbf{X})$, which is simply the average of $\ell_D(\mathbf{x})$ across all $\mathbf{x}$. An analogous decomposition exists for classification problems when the zero‑one loss is used; see Duda et al. [Duda et al. (2000) [@Duda]]. While slightly different in formulation, it carries similar implications regarding the tradeoff between model complexity and generalization error.

---

### Bias Variance Tradeoff

The tradeoff between bias and variance can be visualized using four schematic plots (originally rendered from TikZ files `bullseye_low_low.tex`, `bullseye_high_low.tex`, `bullseye_low_high.tex`, and `bullseye_high_high.tex`). Each plot shows a point in the two‑dimensional space defined by **bias** (horizontal axis) and **variance** (vertical axis):

1. **Low bias – low variance**: Ideal situation where the model fits the data well and generalizes reliably.
2. **High bias – low variance**: The model is too simple; it underfits the data, leading to systematic errors but consistent predictions.
3. **Low bias – high variance**: The model is highly flexible; it captures training data intricacies and is correct on average, but individual predictions vary widely.
4. **High bias – high variance**: Both underfitting and instability are present; performance is poor due to noise and systematic error.

In practice we would like to **minimize both bias and variance**, but for a fixed hypothesis class these two objectives are generally antagonistic. Increasing model capacity tends to reduce bias while simultaneously increasing variance, and vice versa. Consequently, simultaneous optimization of bias and variance is impossible in general. Both quantities can be studied jointly through the notion of **model capacity**, which we discuss next.

---

### Model Capacity

The **capacity** of a model refers to the variety of functions it can represent. While capacity is related to the number of trainable parameters, the relationship is not one‑to‑one: two models with the same parameter count can have very different expressive powers.

A formal measure of capacity is the **Vapnik–Chervonenkis (VC) dimension**. The VC dimension counts the maximum number of points that can be **shattered** (i.e., correctly classified in all possible labelings) by the hypothesis class. Neural networks typically have an extremely large VC dimension compared with classical models such as linear classifiers or nearest‑neighbor rules. The famous experiment that trained a deep network on ImageNet with **random labels** demonstrated that modern architectures can fit arbitrary labelings, suggesting a VC dimension that is effectively unbounded for practical purposes [Zhang et al. (2016) [@randomLabels]]. Hence, the VC dimension alone is insufficient to gauge the *real* capacity of deep networks, especially when regularization and optimization dynamics are taken into account.

Importantly, **increasing capacity always provides a way to reduce bias** because the hypothesis class becomes richer. However, doing so without other controls can dramatically increase variance, leading to overfitting.

---

### The Role of Data

Figure 5 plots **training loss** (red curve) and **test loss** (blue curve) as functions of the number of training samples. As more data become available, both curves decrease, but the test loss plateaus at a higher value than the training loss. This behavior reflects the standard bias–variance intuition:

* **Variance** is primarily reduced by supplying more training examples. With a larger dataset, the learning algorithm can estimate the underlying function more reliably, making the fitted model less sensitive to idiosyncrasies of any particular sample.
* **Bias** is largely governed by model capacity; adding data does not significantly change the bias of a given hypothesis class.

Consequently, an appropriate **match** between model capacity and dataset size is crucial. If the training set is too small relative to the model’s capacity, variance will dominate and the model will overfit. Conversely, if the dataset is large enough, a high‑capacity model can be trained without incurring prohibitive variance. This is why obtaining large, representative datasets is a primary driver of progress in modern machine learning. When obtaining more data is impossible (e.g., due to cost or privacy constraints), other strategies—such as regularization, data augmentation, or incorporating prior knowledge—must be employed to control variance.

---

### Finite Dataset

Figure 6 illustrates the relationship between **model capacity** (horizontal axis) and **loss** (vertical axis) for both training and test sets. As capacity increases:

* The **training loss** (red curve) monotonically decreases, eventually approaching zero. This reflects diminishing bias: a more expressive model can fit the training data ever more closely.
* The **test loss** (blue curve) initially follows the training loss, reaching a minimum at an intermediate capacity. Beyond this point, the test loss rises again, indicating that variance has become dominant and the model is overfitting.

The curves thus visualize the classic bias–variance tradeoff in the capacity domain. For a given problem there typically exists an **optimal capacity** that balances bias and variance, yielding the lowest possible test error. Selecting a model near this optimum is the central goal of regularization.

---

### Regularization reduces Overfitting

Finding the sweet spot on the bias–variance curve can be achieved by **injecting prior knowledge** into the learning process. Prior knowledge can be introduced at several stages:

1. **Data augmentation** – artificially enlarge the training set by applying label‑preserving transformations (e.g., rotations, flips, color jitter), thereby reducing variance.
2. **Architectural design** – choose network architectures that encode useful invariances (e.g., convolutional layers for translation invariance), which constrain the hypothesis space.
3. **Training‑process adaptations** – employ techniques such as early stopping, dropout, or weight decay that penalize overly complex solutions.
4. **Preprocessing** – standardize or normalize inputs to remove irrelevant variability, simplifying the function the model must learn.

These mechanisms correspond to **explicit regularizers** (e.g., equality or inequality constraints on the parameters) or **implicit regularizers** (e.g., architectural biases). By narrowing the set of admissible functions, regularization lowers variance while tolerating a modest increase in bias, ultimately improving generalization on unseen data.

## Classical Techniques

### Overfitting on Learning Curve

During training a model, it is informative to monitor the loss on both the training set and a held‑out test set as a function of the number of optimization iterations.  

*Training‑set loss.*  
The plot of **Loss** versus **Iterations** for the training data shows a rapid decrease of the loss at the beginning of training, followed by a slower decline that eventually plateaus.  This curve reflects the progress of the optimisation algorithm (e.g., stochastic gradient descent) in fitting the model to the training examples.

*Test‑set loss.*  
The analogous plot for the test data (often labelled *TestLoss*) also exhibits an initial rapid decrease, but after a period of stability the loss may start to rise slightly.  The upward drift at the end of training is a hallmark of **overfitting**: the model continues to improve its performance on the training data while its ability to generalise to unseen data degrades.

Two practical conclusions follow:

1. **The test set must never be used for training.** It is reserved exclusively for the final evaluation of a model’s generalisation performance.  
2. **A validation set should be carved out from the training data.** This subset is used during training to tune hyper‑parameters and to decide when to stop training, without contaminating the test set.

---

### Early Stopping

Early stopping exploits the validation‑set loss curve to determine an optimal point at which to halt training.  

The validation loss initially mirrors the training loss: it drops sharply as the model learns useful patterns.  After reaching a minimum, the loss plateaus and then begins to increase modestly, indicating that further optimisation would over‑fit the training data.  A **vertical yellow line** drawn at the iteration corresponding to the lowest validation loss marks the *early‑stopping point*.  

The early‑stopping procedure can be formalised as follows:

1. **Define a stopping criterion.** For example, stop training when the validation loss has not improved for *k* consecutive epochs, or when the loss starts to increase by a predefined margin.  
2. **Select the model parameters** (weights) from the iteration with the **minimum validation loss**.  

By terminating training at this point, we obtain a model that generalises better than the one obtained after the full training schedule.

---

### Data Augmentation

Data augmentation artificially inflates the size of the training set by applying transformations that preserve the semantic label of each example.  The key principle is that *any transformation to which the label should be invariant can be used to generate additional training samples*.

#### Illustrative examples

- **Cat image.** A close‑up photograph of a cat’s face (gray‑white fur, yellow eyes) can be subjected to cropping, slight rotations, colour jittering, or horizontal flips.  None of these operations change the underlying class label “cat”.
- **Digit “6”.** A yellow “6” painted on a dark asphalt surface demonstrates that altering the appearance (e.g., adding noise or changing resolution) does not affect the label (the digit remains a six).  
- **Digit “9” (flipped).** Flipping the image of a “9” horizontally yields another valid instance of the same class, provided that the classifier is trained to be invariant to such spatial transformations.

#### Common transformations

- **Random spatial transformations** (e.g., affine transformations, elastic deformations, rotations, scaling, cropping, flips).  
- **Pixel‑level transformations** (e.g., changing resolution, adding random noise, altering pixel intensity distribution, colour jittering).  

These augmentations expose the model to a broader variety of input appearances, encouraging it to learn features that are robust to irrelevant variations and thus improving generalisation.

### Regularization in the Loss Function

Regularization techniques modify the objective that the learning algorithm minimises.  By adding a penalty term to the standard empirical loss, we can impose prior beliefs about the model parameters (e.g., that they should be small or sparse) and thereby control over‑fitting.

---

### Maximum A Posteriori Estimation

A Bayesian perspective treats the network weights **\( \mathbf{w} \)** as random variables with a prior distribution **\( p(\mathbf{w}) \)**.  Given a dataset **\( \mathbf{X} \)** with associated labels **\( \mathbf{Y} \)**, the joint probability can be factorised as

\[
\begin{align*}
p(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{Y},\mathbf{X}) &= p(\mathbf{w} \mid \mathbf{Y}, \mathbf{X}) \, p(\mathbf{Y} , \mathbf{X})\\
                                      &= p(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w}) \, p(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w}) .
\end{align*}
\]

Applying Bayes’ rule yields the posterior over the weights:

\[
\begin{align*}
p(\mathbf{w} \mid \mathbf{Y}, \mathbf{X}) 
    &= \frac{p(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w}) \, p(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w})}{p(\mathbf{Y},\mathbf{X})}\\[4pt]
    &= \frac{p(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w}) \, p(\mathbf{X}) \, p(\mathbf{w})}{p(\mathbf{Y}\mid \mathbf{X})\,p(\mathbf{X})}.
\end{align*}
\]

Since **\( p(\mathbf{Y}\mid \mathbf{X}) \)** does not depend on **\( \mathbf{w} \)** and **\( \mathbf{X} \)** and **\( \mathbf{X} \)** and **\( \mathbf{w} \)** are independent in the prior, the posterior is proportional to the product of the likelihood and the prior.  Maximising this posterior (Maximum A Posteriori, MAP) is equivalent to minimising a loss function that combines the negative log‑likelihood with a regularisation term derived from **\( -\log p(\mathbf{w}) \)**. This framework allows us to incorporate domain knowledge, such as sparsity priors (e.g., natural images often have sparse gradients), directly into the optimization process.
